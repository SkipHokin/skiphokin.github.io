The Riviera Build

From inspiration to timber, bronze, and a boat slowly becoming real.

This page tells the story of my Glen-L Riviera build, not as a strict timeline, but as a progression from inspiration and intention through structure, engineering, craftsmanship, and the gradual emergence of the boat itself.

Start the Story Go to the Build

The Riviera

The Riviera is one of those designs that carries both elegance and intent. Long foredeck, classic runabout proportions, and just enough engineering complexity to make the build as interesting as the finished boat.

Some projects begin with plans. This one began with places, people, and boats that stayed in the mind long enough to become a commitment.

Inspiration

The inspiration for this build came from a combination of experiences rather than a single moment. Visiting Venice left a strong impression: elegant timber boats, long low lines, and the sense that a beautifully built boat can feel completely at home in its surroundings.

That was reinforced later by attending Glen-L gatherings in Tennessee, where seeing finished boats built by individuals made the idea far more tangible. Standing beside completed boats, talking to the people who built them, and seeing the workmanship up close changes the way you think about what is possible.

Seeing other Rivieras in the flesh helped confirm that this design had exactly the presence I was looking for. The project slowly moved from admiration to intention, and eventually from intention to action.

The Decision to Build

At some point the project shifts from daydream to commitment. Plans are ordered, the workshop starts being viewed differently, and every piece of timber begins to look like it might eventually belong to the boat.

This stage is less dramatic than launching a boat or turning a hull upright, but it matters. It is where the project quietly becomes inevitable.

The Structure Emerges

Every build has a moment where the boat first becomes visible. In this stage the structure begins to define the hull, and what was once abstract starts to take on recognisable shape.

Frames, strongback, alignment, and the first clear hull lines are all part of that transition. There is still a long way to go, but this is where the project begins to look like a boat rather than a set of materials.

The Hull Takes Shape

Once the lines begin to flow from bow to stern, the character of the Riviera becomes much clearer. Chines, battens, fairing, and planking all contribute to that satisfying shift from geometry to form.

This is one of the most visually rewarding parts of the build. The surfaces begin to read as one continuous hull, and the design starts to show its elegance properly.

Engineering Decisions

Boatbuilding is never only woodworking. Mechanical layout, driveline thinking, shaft angles, mounts, brackets, and other engineering choices all shape the eventual character of the boat.

This side of the project is part of the appeal: solving problems, weighing options, and working out how to make the final boat both functional and faithful to the spirit of the build.

Details and Craft

The Riviera rewards detail. Joinery, hardware, fittings, windscreen work, timber finish, and the many smaller decisions all contribute to the overall feel of the boat.

These are often the moments that define the difference between a project that is merely assembled and one that begins to feel crafted.

The Boat as a Whole

Eventually the collection of parts begins to read as one thing. The build moves from workshop tasks and individual assemblies into a clearer sense of the finished boat.

Even before completion, there comes a stage where you can see the Riviera as a whole and recognise that the idea which began with inspiration and observation is now sitting there in timber, shape, and presence.
